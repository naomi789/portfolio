Clarifying School Ratings

Problem statement Each year, over two million parents in the US use ratings on GreatSchools.org to evaluate K-12 school quality - if they misinterpret ratings, they may pick a school they regret. To build users trust, school ratings must be easily understandable.

Overview
  1. Advocating for research During previous research I saw parents misinterpret school ratings. I persuaded stakeholders we should do further research, and recommended prioritized research questions.
  2. Research and impact Create a multi-phase research plan, make recommendations to stakeholders, and launch the product.
  3. Reflections What I do differently going forward.

Advocating for research

During prior user interviews I learned our company was unaware of problems with our school ratings. I gathered these findings and triangulated data from support tickets from our Customer Service team. Using these known usability pain points, I persuaded the design team we should do further research in order to improve the long-term user experience and customer satisfaction. In order to persuade the product team, I focused on the short-term benefits of being able to make design changes (like progressive disclosure) that could increase user interaction and ad revenue.

Referencing referenced foundational research artifacts I had previously created (like personas, journey maps, jobs-to-be-done) I determined my key research questions.

  1. Do users understand that an overall score is calculated using only some of the data on a school's profile?
  2. Do users correctly interpret the information we share?
  3. What follow-up questions do users have about school ratings?
  4. Is this new prototype usable?
Research and impact

Because we planned on making both content changes and design changes, I initially recommended multiple phases of research because I knew that if the content did not make sense, then participants would not want to use the prototype, let alone talk about the design! To run multiple phases of research in a short timeline, I scoped down from our three personas to only speaking with the two essential personas, and chose to only have 4-6 participants each phase.

Phase 1: Progressive disclosure exercise

In order to understand what questions parents needed answered most urgently and how detailed they wanted each answer to be, I started by designing a progressive disclosure exercise. Participants would see basic information about the school (location, grades taught) and how the school was rated on four attributes, and then be invited to ask questions. They could ask their own, or choose from a list of ideas. After reading the answer, they provided feedback and asked follow-up questions.

Impact: Because of these findings, the design team was able to create a prototype that shared a simplified summary for each rating so users could click into each rating if they wanted to hear more about the details.

Phase 2: Comparative prototype evaluation

I tested two different prototypes, each with the new content. I documented what old pain points were persisting from the previous design, and flagged a new pain point.

Impact: The designer drafted a potential solution for the pain point I identified, and we moved on to one final round of testing.

Phase 3: Usability test

In this phase, we explored whether two aspects of the prototype that we preferred, but would be heavier-lift to develop, were essential, or just nice-to-have.

Impact: I recommended cutting one aesthetic design choice, and putting the second heavy-lift feature in the backlog. Users on the prototype without this feature did not find it to be a pain point, but having it was such a delighter for users that we decided that we would aim to release it in late 2025.

Reflections

GreatSchools is best known for being a database of school profiles, and the key part of these features are the school ratings we calculate with our proprietary algorithms. Before starting this project, I wanted to get buy-in that we had the engineering hours to act based on research findings, but it was difficult to talk about that without colleages feeling concerned that I would ask them to make cascading changes: re-writing the algorithm could mean we'd need new blog articles, new marketing content, new social media campaigns.
